Multipolar Security Forum in Moscow: the majority world faces a changing global agenda

The international security forum showed that Russia not only wants to resist Western pressure; it also seeks to position itself as a convening actor for an alternative agenda on stability, sovereignty, and multipolar order.

Javier Salazar Segales General Director
June 1, 2026 17 min read
Global Multipolar Security Forum in Moscow: the majority world faces a changing global agenda Defense and security / Geopolítica

The First International Security Forum held in the Moscow region was more than a technical meeting on global threats. It was a political demonstration. Russia wanted to show that it still retains convening capacity, that Western isolation does not equal global isolation, and that there is a space of countries interested in discussing international security from a perspective different from that of Washington, Brussels, or NATO.

The forum's agenda was broad. It did not stop at military defense or terrorism. It included international security, regional stability, cooperation against transnational threats, cybersecurity, artificial intelligence, critical technologies, water, energy, food, infrastructure, traditional values, neocolonialism, reform of global governance, and the role of the so-called majority world.

The central thesis is clear: Moscow is trying to turn international security into a platform of multipolar legitimacy. This is not only about describing threats. It is about disputing who has the right to define them, which actors take part in that definition, and under what rules global security will be organized.

Security became a language for discussing international order, sovereignty, and global representation.

Putin's message: interdependent security

Vladimir Putin sent a message to the participants and placed the forum within a broader agenda of international cooperation, regional stability, and global security. The gesture was political: to underline that Moscow wants to remain an actor in the conversation about the future of the international system.

The core idea was that security can no longer be understood in isolation. A regional crisis, a technological escalation, a rupture in supply chains, or a food emergency can affect the entire system. Contemporary security is interdependent.

That framing allows Russia to shift the conversation. Moscow does not want the debate to be reduced to Ukraine, sanctions, or confrontation with the West. It seeks to widen it toward transnational threats: terrorism, extremism, organized crime, drug trafficking, nuclear proliferation, cybercrime, information security, and the vulnerability of critical infrastructure.

From technical meeting to strategic platform

The difference between a technical meeting and a strategic forum matters. A technical meeting exchanges diagnoses. An international forum tries to build language, networks, agenda, and legitimacy.

Moscow wants to present this space as a platform where non-Western actors can debate security issues without necessarily passing through institutions dominated by the United States or Europe. This does not amount to creating a full alternative to the UN or the Euro-Atlantic architecture, but it does imply trying to build parallel spaces for strategic conversation.

In that reading, the forum does not function only as a Russian showcase. It works as a rehearsal for a broader political infrastructure: security discussed from other centers, with other emphases and other priorities.

International security is no longer discussed only in Western forums; Moscow wants to be host, moderator, and central actor of an alternative conversation.

The Russian narrative: security versus hegemony

The Russian narrative on security is organized around one central opposition: hegemony versus multipolarity. In Moscow's reading, the order led by the West rests on the expansion of military alliances, sanctions, political pressure, information control, and selective intervention.

Against that, Russia seeks to present an alternative vision based on sovereignty, non-interference, balance of power, indivisible security, and respect for diverse civilizational models. That narrative does not aim first of all to persuade Washington or Brussels, but Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Latin America.

Russian language connects security with neocolonialism, cultural sovereignty, manipulation of narratives, and historical memory. Security, understood in that way, stops being only a matter of weapons and becomes a dispute over the meaning of international order itself.

The majority world and collective responsibility

One of the forum's most relevant concepts was the idea of collective responsibility for world security. The meeting was presented as a space where countries of the majority world recognize inevitable changes in the distribution of power and demand to participate on equal terms in building the new international environment.

This point is central to understanding Russian strategy. Moscow does not want multipolar security to look like an exclusively Russian agenda. It seeks to present it as a shared demand by emerging countries aspiring to greater voice in global governance.

That turns the forum into more than a security meeting. It is also a proposal for order: collective security, not security at others' expense; multipolarity, not unipolar hierarchy; coordination among platforms such as BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organization, not exclusive dependence on Western institutions.

The deeper question is whether the majority world can build a common security agenda without becoming a new rigid bloc.

BRICS and the Global South: coordination with nuances

The forum showed that the discussion of multipolar security is not limited to Russia. The participation of China, India, South Africa, Brazil, Kazakhstan, Vietnam, Malaysia, and other actors helped project a broader narrative on UN reform, sovereignty, technology, collective security, and new rules for international cooperation.

China linked the forum to Xi Jinping's global initiatives on development, security, civilization, and governance. India emphasized UN reform, terrorism, and the need to recognize the new weight of emerging economies. South Africa connected security with AI, cybersecurity, energy, and African integration. Brazil reminded participants that multipolarity must not become a simple division of spheres of influence.

That diversity of positions matters. The forum was not only a Russian platform of denunciation against the West. It was also a space where different actors of the majority world tried to place their own priorities. The potential exists, but it will depend on the ability to turn declarations into projects, institutions, and practical cooperation.

Artificial intelligence and security

One of the strongest axes of contemporary security is artificial intelligence. AI is no longer only an economic or technological tool. It is an infrastructure of power.

It can strengthen defense, border surveillance, data analysis, cybersecurity, and crisis management. But it can also amplify risks: information manipulation, automation of cyberattacks, mass facial recognition, large-scale disinformation, and the falsification of images or videos.

For Russia, China, the United States, Europe, and the Global South, AI is already part of the national security architecture. Whoever controls data, models, computing centers, cloud infrastructure, and analytical systems will have not only an economic advantage, but also a military and informational one.

Twenty-first-century security will depend as much on missiles and armies as on algorithms, satellites, data, and digital platforms.

Fake news, manipulation, and cognitive warfare

The discussion on information security is central in the Russian narrative. Moscow insists that contemporary wars are not fought only on the military field, but also in the minds of societies.

Fake news, deepfakes, coordinated campaigns, emotional manipulation, algorithms that amplify narratives, and different forms of media interference are part of what can be described as cognitive warfare. In that reading, national security includes the ability to protect the population against social destabilization, electoral manipulation, and loss of confidence in institutions.

The problem is that the fight against disinformation creates a dilemma: how to protect the information space without falling into censorship, excessive control, or criminalization of public debate. That dilemma is not only Russian; it also crosses the United States, Europe, China, and other actors.

Satellites, water, food, and biotechnology

The forum also helps us observe a material widening of the security agenda. Satellite platforms such as Starlink and OneWeb show that space connectivity is no longer just civil infrastructure: it is a strategic tool for war, intelligence, communications, and emergency operations.

To this we must add fresh water, food, fertilizers, biotechnology, and public health. Climate crises, wars, sanctions, and trade restrictions can turn these sectors into areas of strategic dispute. Kazakhstan's proposal to promote an international water security organization is a clear sign of this shift.

Food, water, and biological security are no longer secondary agendas. They are being integrated into the core of national security, together with biosecurity, agricultural chains, health technologies, and critical infrastructure.

Europe and the dispute over legitimacy

From the Russian narrative, Europe is undergoing a strategic transformation marked by militarization, higher defense spending, migration pressure, and a crisis of social cohesion. Moscow interprets that dynamic as a sign of strategic subordination to the United States and of internal civilizational fragility.

That approach can be disputed from the West, but it has analytical value because it shows how Russia tries to contest the narrative about Europe. For Moscow, European security is not measured only by tanks and defense budgets, but also by internal cohesion, political legitimacy, cultural identity, and strategic autonomy.

Beyond the normative validity of that discourse, its political function is clear: to show that security is also played out in the internal legitimacy of models of order, not only in military balance.

Implications for Latin America

Latin America should observe forums like this with pragmatism. The region does not need to adopt the Russian narrative or align itself with Moscow. But neither can it ignore that global security is becoming more plural and that several centers of power are competing to define which threats matter and which responses are legitimate.

For the region, this has concrete implications: cybersecurity, transnational crime, drug trafficking, artificial intelligence, critical infrastructure, energy, defense, sanctions, information, water, food, and military cooperation.

The strategic question is whether Latin America can build an autonomous security policy capable of speaking with different centers of power without becoming subordinate to their agendas. If the region does not define its priorities, other actors will do it for it.

Multipolarity only expands room for those who arrive with an agenda of their own.

Possible scenarios

1. Consolidation of alternative security forums

Russia keeps events of this type as recurring platforms for non-Western countries. They do not replace existing global institutions, but they do function as parallel channels of strategic dialogue.

2. Fragmented multipolar security

Parallel security spaces emerge: NATO and the European Union on one side; Russia, China, the SCO, BRICS, and other formats on the other; with Global South countries moving between both without full alignment.

3. Security diplomacy of the Global South

Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Latin America use multiple platforms to maximize autonomy. They do not accept a single security agenda, but negotiate case by case according to national interests.

4. AI, cybersecurity, and satellites as new fields of dispute

The security agenda shifts toward data, artificial intelligence, satellite systems, cognitive warfare, critical infrastructure, and control of narratives. Security forums stop being only military spaces.

5. Biological, food, and water security as priority

Water, food, biotechnology, fertilizers, public health, and biosecurity become central fields of competition and cooperation. States seek to protect vital chains against hybrid and climate crises.

Conclusion

Moscow's International Security Forum was more than a meeting of experts. It was an attempt by Russia to reinsert itself as an organizing actor in the global debate on security. Putin's message, the Russian agenda, and the presence of actors from the majority world point in the same direction: to dispute the very definition of international security.

But the forum also showed something broader. Multipolar security is not an exclusively Russian agenda. China, India, South Africa, Brazil, Kazakhstan, Malaysia, and other actors put their own priorities on the table: UN reform, multilateralism, the fight against terrorism, AI, cybersecurity, water, energy, regional integration, new technologies, and multipolarity without rigid spheres of influence.

The question is not whether Moscow can replace the Western security architecture by itself. It cannot. The question is whether these spaces can become sufficiently attractive platforms for the Global South to use them as instruments of negotiation, diversification, and autonomy.

Open questions

  • Is Russia building an alternative security architecture or a platform of resistance vis-à-vis the West?
  • Can the Global South benefit from these forums without being trapped in rivalries among major powers?
  • Will multipolar security increase the autonomy of emerging countries or multiply pressures on them?
  • Can Latin America define its own strategic threats without importing external agendas?
